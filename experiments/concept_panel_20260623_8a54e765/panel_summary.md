## Facilitator Audit Patterns

- Coverage was completed before the facilitator isolated what the concept would actually replace, what behavior it would change, and which claims remained hypothetical rather than observed. across 1 personas: Maggie Lau
  Assessment: Coverage sequencing was efficient and conversational, but depth was too shallow at several key moments where one additional contrast or consequence probe would have materially improved evidence quality.
- The main failure mode was accepting first-pass hypothetical reactions without enough contrast, threshold, or current-workflow comparison probing, then treating those reactions as stronger proof than the transcript supports. across 1 personas: Ivy Chan
  Assessment: Coverage was prioritized over depth. Required areas were touched, but several high-signal participant clues were not unpacked before moving to the next coverage item.

## Common Missed High-Value Follow-Ups

- [trust_condition_signal] Can you think of a time a tool or report failed that explanation standard, and what did you do instead? across 1 personas: Ivy Chan
  Learning: Trust requirements become more actionable when contrasted with a failure example, not just a stated preference list.
- [participant gave a repeat-use condition bundle] If only one of those were true on the first few uses, which one would matter most for you to come back? across 1 personas: Maggie Lau
  Learning: When participants name multiple retention conditions, ask them to rank or break ties so the strongest driver is not lost.
- [participant described a hypothetical next step after a tool alert] Thinking back to the event you described earlier, if this tool had existed then, what exactly would it have changed in what you did, if anything? across 1 personas: Maggie Lau
  Learning: Convert hypothetical tool reactions back into a recalled event comparison to distinguish time-saving from true behavior change.
- [volunteered_workaround_protection] What does that delayed comparison protect you from, and can you recall a time when that pause changed what you would have done? across 1 personas: Ivy Chan
  Learning: When a participant describes a workaround cadence, probe what risk the workaround protects against and whether it has ever changed an action.
- [timing_preference_signal] What happens if the same signal arrives outside that moment, and how would you handle it differently? across 1 personas: Ivy Chan
  Learning: When a participant states a preferred moment, use a contrast probe to learn why other moments fail and what behavior changes across contexts.
- [threshold_signal] What would count as far enough off that you would move from waiting to acting, and can you anchor that to a recent real case? across 1 personas: Ivy Chan
  Learning: Whenever a participant uses threshold language such as 'only if it is obvious' or 'too much,' ask for the decision threshold and anchor it to a specific case.
- [participant described a real action after a recent event] What would have had to be different for you to make a bigger change, or for you to leave everything untouched? across 1 personas: Maggie Lau
  Learning: After a recalled action, use threshold or contrast probes to understand the minimum condition for different behavior.
- [repeat_use_claim] What would make you stop opening it after the first few uses even if it seemed useful at first? across 1 personas: Ivy Chan
  Learning: Retention claims should be tested with drop-off and disappointment probes, not only success-condition probes.
- [participant said the concept would be only a quick overview] What would you still need to check after that overview before you would trust yourself to act? across 1 personas: Maggie Lau
  Learning: When a participant narrows a concept to a lightweight role, follow with a boundary probe to learn what the tool would not replace.
- [participant rejected vague scores and sales-like flow] Which part would make you close it first: the vague explanation, the request for more steps, or the feeling that it is steering you somewhere? across 1 personas: Maggie Lau
  Learning: When multiple abandonment reasons are volunteered together, isolate the first-break condition instead of keeping them bundled.

## Common Likely Misclassified Drivers

- The participant's request for explanations was treated mainly as a feature trust requirement. -> The deeper driver may be preserving decision autonomy and resisting opaque external steering. across 1 personas: Ivy Chan
  Learning: When participants ask for explanation and defer human involvement, consider whether the real driver is decision control rather than simple clarity.
- The participant seemed mainly sensitive to trust and onboarding friction. -> The deeper issue may have been skepticism toward tools that ask for commitment before proving immediate relevance. across 1 personas: Maggie Lau
  Learning: When participants mention setup friction, test whether the real barrier is effort, premature commitment, or lack of immediate proof of value.
- The participant seemed to want a portfolio-health feature that summarizes accounts. -> The more fundamental driver may have been reducing uncertainty before acting under time or consequence pressure, with summary only serving that broader control need. across 1 personas: Maggie Lau
  Learning: Do not equate enthusiasm for summary or visibility with demand for a feature; probe what risk or uncertainty that summary helps manage.
- The participant's current behavior was treated mainly as a lightweight review habit. -> The stronger driver may be self-protection against impulsive action under uncertainty, not just preference for convenience. across 1 personas: Ivy Chan
  Learning: When a participant describes a repeated manual ritual, check whether it protects against a cognitive or emotional failure mode before classifying it as mere friction or habit.
- The participant seemed suitable for passive in-flow exposure rather than active exploration. -> The stronger pattern may be situational relevance: people engage when a current decision makes the information consequential, regardless of channel placement. across 1 personas: Maggie Lau
  Learning: Do not treat preferred placement as a stable adoption driver until you separate context timing from navigation convenience.
