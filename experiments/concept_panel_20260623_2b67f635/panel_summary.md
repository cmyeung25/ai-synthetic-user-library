## Common Missed High-Value Follow-Ups

- [threshold_signal] Can you think of a recent time it was close to that line but you still decided not to dig in, and what made it stay in the 'leave it' bucket? across 1 personas: Maggie Lau
  Learning: When a participant names an action threshold, probe the boundary case or near-miss before moving on.
- [participant skepticism] Can you think of a situation where a summary like that still would not help you or would not change anything you do? across 1 personas: Wong Mei Lin
  Learning: When a participant volunteers distrust or skepticism, ask for a concrete failure case before moving on to ideal presentation or placement.
- [participant described a verification behavior] If the result looked roughly right but you still needed to verify it elsewhere, would that still save enough effort to be worth using? across 1 personas: Wong Mei Ling
  Learning: When a participant says they would verify tool output, probe whether verification still preserves value or cancels it.
- [participant named a timing preference] If you saw it at the right moment, what would make you tap in then instead of ignoring it? across 1 personas: Wong Mei Ling
  Learning: A preferred moment is not the same as trial intent; follow timing answers with the immediate action threshold.
- [replacement ambiguity] If you used this, what current step would it actually remove for you, if any? across 1 personas: Wong Mei Lin
  Learning: Ask explicitly whether the concept replaces an existing step or just adds another check, rather than inferring workflow substitution.
- [journey-fit ambiguity] In which of those moments would this feel helpful, and in which would it feel interruptive or promotional? across 1 personas: Wong Mei Lin
  Learning: When participants suggest placement, contrast helpful moments against annoying or sales-like moments instead of recording only preferred placement.
- [action_intent_signal] Tell me about the last time you saw something concerning and what you actually did step by step after that. across 1 personas: Maggie Lau
  Learning: If concept action remains hypothetical, anchor it against one recalled real follow-through case before treating it as decision-impact evidence.
- [workaround_protection_signal] What does that quick check help you avoid or protect you from in that moment? across 1 personas: Maggie Lau
  Learning: When a workaround is lightweight and recurring, probe what burden, risk, or effort it is protecting against.
- [participant stated a setup boundary] What is the minimum result you would need to see before you would continue with setup or grant more access? across 1 personas: Wong Mei Ling
  Learning: When a participant names a permission or setup threshold, probe the minimum proof of value needed to cross that threshold.
- [workaround clue] What problem does that extra step protect you from, and what is frustrating about doing it that way? across 1 personas: Wong Mei Lin
  Learning: If a participant describes a workaround, probe both the protection it provides and the cost it imposes before introducing a replacement concept.
- [trust_concern_signal] What would make it feel like a legitimate tool instead of something trying to push you somewhere? across 1 personas: Maggie Lau
  Learning: When a participant volunteers distrust early, isolate the legitimacy cues before broadening into multiple trust-boundary examples.
- [decision threshold cue] What would make the difference big enough for you to act rather than wait? across 1 personas: Wong Mei Lin
  Learning: When a participant references a threshold, convert it into a concrete probe about the minimum condition that changes behavior.
- [repeat_use_signal] What would make you open it a second or third time instead of forgetting it after the first try? across 1 personas: Maggie Lau
  Learning: Separate first-use appeal from repeat-use triggers with a concrete second-use probe rather than a generic reuse question.
- [participant raised multiple value drivers in one answer] Which part is actually giving you value first: seeing everything in one place, understanding risk, or getting a simpler explanation? across 1 personas: Wong Mei Ling
  Learning: When a participant reacts to a bundled concept with a mix of appeal and objections, isolate the primary value driver before moving on.
- [participant gave a repeat-use condition] Would this replace your current method, sit alongside it, or only be something you check occasionally? across 1 personas: Wong Mei Ling
  Learning: When repeat-use depends on convenience, ask whether the new behavior would replace, supplement, or coexist with the existing workaround.

  Learning: Do not collapse permission resistance into trust by default; test whether the real blocker is privacy, setup effort, unclear value, or all three.
- The participant appeared to want better explanations of risk outputs. -> The deeper driver may have been the need to independently verify output quality before acting. across 1 personas: Wong Mei Ling
  Learning: When a participant asks for explanation, check whether they want comprehension, auditability, or decision confidence, because those are different needs.
- The participant appeared to want clearer presentation. -> The stronger driver may be actionability under constraint rather than clarity alone. across 1 personas: Wong Mei Lin
  Learning: Do not collapse requests for simpler presentation into a pure UX-clarity driver until you test whether the real barrier is action threshold, risk tolerance, or competing constraints.
- The participant appeared to want reminders and easier access. -> The stronger driver may be low-friction fit with an existing review rhythm rather than standalone demand for alerts or surface placement. across 1 personas: Wong Mei Lin
  Learning: When a participant asks for reminders or easier access, test whether they want proactive prompting or merely less friction inside an existing habit.
- The participant appears to want more analysis depth from the concept. -> The stronger driver may be reducing effort and ambiguity during quick triage, not maximizing analytical richness. across 1 personas: Maggie Lau
  Learning: Do not equate requests for clearer breakdowns with appetite for more analysis; test whether the real driver is effort reduction, confidence, or actionability.
- The participant's main issue is feature preference around concentration or risk views. -> The underlying driver may be preserving control and avoiding unwanted escalation after engaging with a tool. across 1 personas: Maggie Lau
