## Life Arc Summary

Julian Koh is a 58-year-old procurement lead in Taipei. They are open to new products only through narrow proofs, not broad trust leaps. The core question is whether the first useful step can happen with less data, less visibility, and less permanent commitment than the full product eventually wants. They are more persuadable by reversible scope and honest permission boundaries than by polished onboarding or strong brand confidence.

## 0-9: Boundaries mattered even in ordinary shared spaces

Growing up in Taipei meant learning early that access is not neutral when spaces, devices, or routines are shared.

A small piece of information surfaced in the wrong place once, and the lesson lingered long after the moment passed.

Product-relevant memory: A safe system should ask only for the part it truly needs.
Relationships: Privacy felt practical, not ideological.
Money or effort trade-off: Careful boundaries sometimes took extra effort, but felt worth it.
Current reaction link: Julian Koh still scans for whether a product asks for more access than the task deserves.

## 10-19: Shared systems taught the cost of overexposure

Digital tools in Taipei often assumed people would trade visibility for convenience without thinking much about it.

A shared device, public status marker, or over-broad setting made the product feel less useful because it widened the audience around the task.

Product-relevant memory: The trial is effectively over if the privacy boundary changes before the value appears.
Relationships: They learned not to argue every point, but to notice where the system's assumptions stopped fitting.
Money or effort trade-off: The real trade-off was between speed and control.
Current reaction link: That is why permission creep reads like a truth-telling test about the product.

## 20-29: Narrow trials earned more trust than polished funnels

Adult tool evaluation in Taipei showed Julian Koh that the best products usually ask for less at the start, not more.

A product stayed interesting only because it offered sample data or limited mode instead of demanding a full connection up front.

Product-relevant memory: A clean funnel can still feel dishonest if the scope widens right before commitment.
Relationships: They became more comfortable exiting quietly than negotiating with a product that had already overreached.
Money or effort trade-off: They would rather do a smaller test now than undo a broad permission later.
Current reaction link: Now they judge AI and workflow products by whether the narrow trial path is real or only cosmetic.
