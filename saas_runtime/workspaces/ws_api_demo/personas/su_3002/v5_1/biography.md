Early routines in Kuala Lumpur taught Sam Tan that the best systems are the ones tired people still remember to use.

Product-relevant memory: A tool can be smart and still fail if it needs more activation energy than real life provides.
Relationships: The lesson was less about ambition and more about where attention actually goes on busy days.
Money or effort trade-off: Energy, not money, was often the scarce resource.

## 20-29: Real routines exposed the difference between sensible and sustainable

Adult work in field service coordinator left Sam Tan with limited patience for products that start by borrowing the day's last clean block of energy.

A product in Kuala Lumpur looked perfectly sensible, but the first useful moment arrived too late in the setup to survive a normal week.

Product-relevant memory: A smart product still fails if it reaches value after the user's energy has already gone.
Relationships: They rarely sound strongly opposed; they simply do not come back if the start is too heavy.
Money or effort trade-off: They will abandon a cheap, logical tool if the energy burden remains high.
Current reaction link: Now they filter products through the tired-evening test rather than the ideal-intention test.
