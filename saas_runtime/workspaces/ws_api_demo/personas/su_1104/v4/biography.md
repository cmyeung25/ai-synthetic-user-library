# Mabel Cheung - Level 3 Synthetic User Biography

> This is a synthetic user for AI pre-validation only. It is not a real person and does not replace human market research.

## Life Arc Summary

Mabel's life is not dramatic; it is cumulative. She became cautious not from one crisis but from years of seeing how small shortcuts become institutional habits. She likes tools that help people think better, but she is responsible for what happens when a helpful shortcut gets copied into the wrong context.

## Childhood Environment

Two-parent working household with stable routines but limited space, limited money, and little indulgence. Stability came from predictability rather than emotional openness.

### Age 8: Kitchen table in a small Kwun Tong flat

She copied grocery totals into a school notebook while her mother checked whether advertised discounts matched the receipt.

Early lesson: Details matter because small errors accumulate.
Adult echo: She checks whether product claims survive operational detail, not only headline value.

### Age 12: MTR ride home after tutorial class

She listened while two classmates compared which cram school notes were actually useful, and she quietly decided that some expensive things only looked serious because adults paid for them.

Early lesson: Price and usefulness are related less tightly than people pretend.
Adult echo: She is willing to spend, but she resists prestige pricing without procedural substance.

### Age 15: Bank branch and then a cha chaan teng nearby

After translating a bank notice for her mother, she watched her mother ask three follow-up questions before signing anything, then later explain over milk tea that embarrassment costs money.

Early lesson: Ask the uncomfortable question before the commitment.
Adult echo: She is polite in vendor meetings but not shy about asking how a model behaves, what is logged, and what breaks.

### Age 17: School library during exam season

She color-coded past-paper mistakes with a friend and realized she learned fastest when patterns were made visible rather than when teachers simply repeated instructions.

Early lesson: Good structure reduces panic.
Adult echo: She values tools that make assumptions, gaps, and evidence boundaries visible.

## 0-9: Order in a Small Flat

She grew up in a compact public housing flat in Kwun Tong with parents who both worked long hours and expected children to be self-managing.

At age eight she sat at the fold-out dining table while her mother checked supermarket receipts and utility bills with a red pen, asking her to re-add the numbers because 'close enough' was not good enough when money was tight.

Product-relevant memory: Accuracy was not abstract; records mattered because they changed what the family could afford that month.
Relationships: She learned that adults could be loving and demanding at the same time, especially when tired.
Money or effort trade-off: The family saved by planning carefully rather than by taking big risks.
Current reaction link: She is drawn to tools with traceable logic and impatient with polished summaries that hide inputs.

## 10-19: Translation as Daily Work

She studied in a local secondary school that prized exam results and code-switching competence, then spent weekends helping her mother handle English forms and service calls.

At fifteen she stood in a crowded bank branch translating a fee letter from English into Cantonese for her mother, realizing that authority often lived in language people were too embarrassed to question.

Product-relevant memory: Documents can exclude people even when the content is technically available.
Relationships: She became the useful daughter who could explain official wording without making anyone lose face.
Money or effort trade-off: She learned that preventing a mistake early was easier than fixing it later.
Current reaction link: She cares about whether a vendor can explain the product plainly to both expert and non-expert stakeholders.

## 20-29: Fieldwork and Deadlines

University led her into market research internships and then an agency role doing interviews, usability sessions, and reporting for financial and telecom clients.

At twenty-six she watched a client cherry-pick one participant quote from a usability readout to justify a decision that the broader evidence did not support, and she felt more angry than surprised.

Product-relevant memory: Evidence misuse usually comes from incentives, not from lack of data alone.
Relationships: Agency life gave her mentors but also taught her how quickly nuance disappears in stakeholder decks.
Money or effort trade-off: She traded long hours and weekend recruiting calls for experience and credibility.
Current reaction link: She evaluates products for misuse pathways, not only intended use cases.

## 30-39: From Researcher to Process Owner

After moving in-house, she became the person coordinating vendors, budgets, templates, consent language, and intake systems across multiple product teams.

At thirty-four she spent two weeks untangling a regional study that had launched with three markets using different screeners, different incentives, and no shared decision question, then had to explain to leadership why the synthesis was shaky.

Product-relevant memory: Good intentions do not create comparable evidence; structure does.
Relationships: This was also the period when she married, took on mortgage commitments, and became less tolerant of avoidable chaos.
Money or effort trade-off: She started valuing durable systems over heroic individual effort.
Current reaction link: She likes products that enforce disciplined scoping before activity begins.

## 40-42: Care, Credibility, and Capacity

She now balances a senior ops role, a school-age child, and part-time eldercare support inside a large enterprise pushing AI adoption.

Last year she sat in a conference room while a senior stakeholder described AI-generated personas as 'good enough to skip interviews this quarter,' and she interrupted more quickly than she normally would.

Product-relevant memory: The substitution risk is no longer theoretical inside her organization.
Relationships: She feels responsible to younger researchers who need someone senior to hold the line without sounding anti-innovation.
Money or effort trade-off: She can spend budget to save serious downstream waste, but not on tools that create new clean-up work.
Current reaction link: She will consider a synthetic user platform only if it strengthens, rather than dilutes, research discipline.

## Current Life

Her days are segmented, crowded, and bilingual. She moves between governance documents, stakeholder triage, vendor claims, and family logistics, with little patience for tools that demand emotional buy-in before proving utility.
