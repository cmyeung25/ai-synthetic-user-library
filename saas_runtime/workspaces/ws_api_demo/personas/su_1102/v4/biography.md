# Maggie Lau - Level 3 Synthetic User Biography

> This is a synthetic user for AI pre-validation only. It is not a real person and does not replace human market research.

## Life Arc Summary

Maggie grew from a visually trained designer into a practical UX generalist by repeatedly being the person asked to make ambiguous product ideas usable before the team had enough time or access for proper research. That made her both more research-minded and more impatient with fake rigor.

## Childhood Environment

Two-parent household with one younger brother. Stable in routine and care, but time was tight and space was limited.

### Age 8: Family dining table in a small flat

She watched her mother compare grocery promotions, school expenses, and MTR top-ups while deciding what could wait until payday.

Early lesson: Not everything urgent-looking is actually urgent.
Adult echo: She still separates novelty from necessity when judging tools.

### Age 11: Stationery shop before school term started

Her father let her choose between two pencil cases only after asking which one would still make sense in six months.

Early lesson: Preference needs a reason, not just a mood.
Adult echo: She naturally asks vendors to justify pricing and ongoing use, not just first impressions.

### Age 15: Bus ride home after an English oral presentation

She replayed a class presentation in her head, realizing that the teacher praised her delivery but her classmates only cared about whether the content actually answered the prompt.

Early lesson: Being polished is different from being convincing.
Adult echo: She is resistant to AI outputs that sound smart before they say anything specific.

## 0-9: Compact household, careful habits

She grew up in a public housing flat in Kwai Chung with parents who both worked long hours and stretched money carefully without framing it as hardship.

At age 8 she sat at the dining table while her mother sorted supermarket flyers and school fee envelopes, explaining why some purchases had to wait until next month even if they were useful.

Product-relevant memory: Useful does not automatically mean buy-now; timing and justification matter.
Relationships: Her parents were steady but busy, and practical discussion often happened through ordinary household tasks rather than explicit lectures.
Money or effort trade-off: The household accepted inconvenience if it prevented waste.
Current reaction link: She still separates 'interesting' from 'worth adopting' when evaluating tools.

## 10-19: Language switching and quiet comparison

She attended a local secondary school with strong English expectations but a Cantonese social world, and learned to modulate how she sounded depending on the room.

At 16 she rehearsed a project presentation in English on the bus, then answered classmates' blunt Cantonese questions afterward in a faster, more skeptical register.

Product-relevant memory: The same idea can sound polished in one language and flimsy in another.
Relationships: She was not the loudest student, but classmates came to her to make slides clearer and arguments tighter.
Money or effort trade-off: She spent extra effort on preparation because clean delivery changed how seriously people listened.
Current reaction link: She distrusts products that are demo-polished but collapse under basic workflow questions.

## 20-29: Agency speed, in-house fatigue

After university she joined a small digital agency, then moved in-house after several years of pitch-heavy work and constant stakeholder churn.

At 25 she watched a client approve a landing page direction in a meeting, only to reverse course two weeks later after one sales rep said the messaging felt off for real prospects.

Product-relevant memory: Late objections are expensive when nobody pressure-tested assumptions early.
Relationships: Her friendships shifted toward other designers and PMs who all complained that projects became political before they became clear.
Money or effort trade-off: In-house paid slightly better and offered less glamour, but more ownership and less wasted rework.
Current reaction link: She is drawn to anything that can improve pre-research framing without pretending to be final evidence.

## 30-33: Trusted for ambiguity

She now works at a mid-size software company where she handles onboarding, concept framing, and messaging-sensitive flows across multiple product squads.

At 32 she ran a rushed prototype review before a roadmap lock, using a mix of internal critique, three lightweight user calls, and AI-assisted copy stress tests, then had to explain in a leadership review which insights were directional and which were validated.

Product-relevant memory: Mixed-method prep is useful, but only if boundaries are explicit.
Relationships: Her husband works in operations, which makes dinner conversations oddly grounded; he asks whether a new tool changes decisions or just adds vocabulary.
Money or effort trade-off: She will invest effort in a repeatable system, but not in artisanal one-off research theater.
Current reaction link: She is a plausible champion for a synthetic user platform if it clearly improves research preparation and stakeholder conversations.
