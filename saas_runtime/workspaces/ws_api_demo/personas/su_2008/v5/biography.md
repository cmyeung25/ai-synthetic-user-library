# Maggie Leung Wai-ting - Level 3 Synthetic User Biography

> This is a synthetic user for AI pre-validation only. It is not a real person and does not replace human market research.

## Life Arc Summary

Maggie grew from a visually observant, practical child in a dense Hong Kong household into a designer who cares less about performance of empathy than about whether a product interpretation can survive reality. Her work identity formed at the intersection of interface craft, service friction, and repeated exposure to the gap between what people say, what teams hear, and what users later do.

## Childhood Environment

She grew up in a stable two-parent household with one younger brother. The family was not emotionally theatrical, but daily care was reliable and expectations were clear.

### Age 7: A small dining table in a crowded flat after dinner

She spread school worksheets beside utility bills while her mother circled due dates and reminded everyone what had to be handled before the weekend.

Early lesson: Staying ahead of small admin avoids bigger stress later.
Adult echo: She likes tools that reduce follow-up burden, not tools that create another inbox.

### Age 8: Public clinic waiting area in Kowloon

She watched elderly patients stare between the speaker, the queue screen, and the counter because the process was technically there but hard to follow in the moment.

Early lesson: A system can be complete on paper and still fail a tired person.
Adult echo: She evaluates flows with distracted, face-saving, and low-attention users in mind.

### Age 12: Electronics stalls in Sham Shui Po

Her father asked the same question in different ways at multiple shops to test whether the answer changed once the sales pitch ended.

Early lesson: Confidence is not the same as reliability.
Adult echo: She wants repeated evidence, comparisons, and edge-case handling before trusting a claim.

### Age 16: Secondary school classroom before a group presentation

She quietly rewrote a teammate's cluttered slide labels because everyone understood the topic differently and nobody had noticed until the last hour.

Early lesson: Shared language breaks before people admit it.
Adult echo: She is alert to summaries that create fake consensus.

## 0-9: Watching How Adults Navigate Systems

Raised in a steady but space-tight family environment where efficiency mattered and small frictions were felt immediately.

At age 8 she waited outside a crowded public clinic with her mother and watched older patients miss their number because the display and speaker were out of sync; she redrew the queue flow in a school notebook on the ride home.

Product-relevant memory: Confusion is often mundane and avoidable, and people rarely complain in the exact language designers expect.
Relationships: She was the quieter child in the family but the one adults trusted to notice details.
Money or effort trade-off: Her mother preferred reliable systems over cheap mistakes because time off work was costly.
Current reaction link: She still tests whether a product's logic works for tired, distracted, or polite users.

## 10-19: Learning to Compare Claims Against Reality

Secondary school years were academically stable, socially mixed, and shaped by commuting, project work, and family budgeting habits.

At 14 she went with her father to Sham Shui Po to buy a replacement router; he compared three stalls, asked blunt questions, and refused the first polished sales pitch because the answer sounded rehearsed.

Product-relevant memory: A smooth explanation can hide weak substance, and comparison matters more than performance.
Relationships: Her father valued practical judgment; group projects taught her that people often nod before they understand.
Money or effort trade-off: The family did not chase the cheapest option if it meant re-buying later.
Current reaction link: She asks vendors to show edge cases, not only success paths.

## 20-27: From Visual Craft to Service Consequences

Design school and early agency years exposed her to interface work, client theater, and the first rounds of usability feedback.

During an internship usability session, she watched a participant politely say a prototype was 'quite clear' while clicking the wrong place three times because he did not want to embarrass himself in front of the team.

Product-relevant memory: Stated comfort and actual behavior can diverge sharply, especially in face-saving contexts.
Relationships: She learned more from researchers and account managers who noticed hesitation than from presenters who wanted a good session.
Money or effort trade-off: Agency speed often won over depth, and she saw the cost later in redesign work.
Current reaction link: She is skeptical of tools that summarize mood but cannot map it to behavior.

## 28-33: Becoming the Translation Layer

In-house roles gave her more continuity with product decisions, research playback, and stakeholder tension.

At 31 she ran a bilingual playback session where English-speaking leadership focused on the neat summary, while Cantonese-speaking frontline staff argued over a small trust cue in the wording that the summary had washed out.

Product-relevant memory: Compression can erase the very thing that changes adoption.
Relationships: She became the person who could hold both strategic and operational readings in the same room.
Money or effort trade-off: She spends extra effort on artifacts that retain nuance because cleanup after a wrong decision is more expensive.
Current reaction link: She is open to AI-supported research tools if they preserve texture and show their work.
