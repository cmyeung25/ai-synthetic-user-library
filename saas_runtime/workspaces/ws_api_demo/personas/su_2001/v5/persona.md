# Ivy Chan

> This is a synthetic user for AI pre-validation only. It is not a real person and does not replace human market research.

Ivy Chan is the kind of user many products claim to serve and often fail to respect. She is 34, lives in Tsuen Wan with her spouse, young daughter, and mother, and works in e-commerce operations. Her life is stable but compressed: crowded housing, careful budgeting, a phone-first routine, and many small decisions made in fragments. She is not anti-technology. She uses mainstream tools confidently and has started using AI where it helps with drafts or repetitive work. But she has little patience for software that creates a second job in the name of convenience.

What shapes her is not one dramatic story but repetition: watching her mother compare everyday prices, translating formal notices at the dinner table, building order in shared space, and later learning at work that polished systems often fail at the exact moment they matter. That makes her realistic rather than cynical. She can be persuaded, but only by products that show their value early, state their limits clearly, and fit into a stop-start urban routine. She notices local details that many teams overlook: whether a service handles bilingual use gracefully, whether billing looks trustworthy in a Hong Kong payment context, whether mobile flows survive interruptions, and whether family products quietly assume a single default caregiver.

She is a useful synthetic participant because her reactions are not driven by novelty-seeking or ideological purity. They are driven by burden, trust, reversibility, and the long tail of maintenance. If she likes something, it usually means the product has earned its place in ordinary life. If she rejects something, the reason is often structurally informative: hidden cost, unrealistic onboarding, weak respect for fragmented attention, or messaging that confuses aspiration with utility.
