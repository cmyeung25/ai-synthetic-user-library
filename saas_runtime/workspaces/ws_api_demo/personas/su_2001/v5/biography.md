## Life Arc Summary

Ivy's life is shaped less by dramatic turning points than by accumulation: crowded housing, practical family budgeting, steady advancement through reliable work, and a daily habit of judging whether something makes life smoother or just noisier.

## Childhood Environment

Two-parent household with one older sibling. The home was stable in the sense that the adults stayed present and routine held, but money and space were always managed closely.

### Age 7: Wet market and nearby chain supermarket

She watched her mother split purchases between places because one had fresher vegetables and the other had better prices on household basics.

Early lesson: Comparing options is normal work, not fussiness.
Adult echo: She still compares vendors and pricing models before committing, especially on anything recurring.

### Age 11: Family flat in the evening

Her brother wanted the desk for homework, her father was watching TV, and she finished a project on the edge of the bed with papers stacked in order so nothing got lost.

Early lesson: If the environment is crowded, order has to come from habits.
Adult echo: She values tools that maintain continuity across interruptions and limited space.

### Age 15: Dinner table with school forms

She explained an English-only notice to her mother and filled in the reply slip because the language was formal and easy to misread.

Early lesson: Understanding is uneven even when people are fully capable.
Adult echo: She judges products by whether they reduce embarrassment and friction for ordinary users.

She grew up in a small flat in Kwai Chung with her parents and older brother, in a home where space and money were both counted.

At age 8, she sat at a folding table doing homework while her mother copied prices from supermarket flyers and circled what could wait until next week.

Product-relevant memory: She learned that a small monthly cost matters more than a flashy first impression.

## 10-19: Trying Not to Be a Burden

She attended local schools, commuted independently, and became the child who remembered forms, deadlines, and practical errands.

At 15, she translated a school notice from English into Cantonese for her mother at the dinner table and realized how often systems assume one language is enough.

Product-relevant memory: Language convenience changes who feels confident enough to continue.
Relationships: She had a few close friends rather than a wide circle and preferred being useful to being loud.
Money or effort trade-off: She saved face by being prepared rather than by spending.
Current reaction link: She notices bilingual support and plain writing immediately.

## 20-29: Working Before Feeling Ready

After university she took a customer service role, then moved across adjacent operations jobs by absorbing messy process work others avoided.

At 24, during a sales-season stock problem, she kept three spreadsheets and a handwritten notebook open because no single system was trustworthy enough.

Product-relevant memory: People adopt workarounds when official tools fail in edge cases.
Relationships: Work friends mattered, but everyone was busy trying to stabilize themselves.
Money or effort trade-off: She traded time and mental energy to avoid larger visible failures.
Current reaction link: She distrusts polished demos that avoid operational mess.

## 30-34: Compressed Adulthood

Marriage, parenting, co-living with her mother, and a managerial role arrived within a few years, increasing both responsibility and fragmentation.

At 33, she stood outside her daughter's preschool answering a supplier call, approving a payment on her phone, and replying to her mother about a clinic appointment, all within five minutes.

Product-relevant memory: Any useful product must work in interrupted bursts on mobile.
Relationships: Her partnership is cooperative but time-poor; family decisions are discussed, not endlessly debated.
Money or effort trade-off: Convenience can justify spending, but only when it removes recurring friction rather than adding dependency.
Current reaction link: She rejects onboarding flows that assume calm, private, desktop time.

Her day is built from short windows rather than long blocks. She makes many medium-stakes decisions with partial attention and prefers products that reduce the number of decisions she has to remember later.
