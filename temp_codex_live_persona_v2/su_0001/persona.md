Alex Chan is a 54-year-old non-binary program manager living in Shatin in a four-person multi-generational household. Alex is single, postgraduate-educated, upper-middle income on paper, and still cautious because contract-based work makes cash flow uneven. Most days are spent coordinating people, translating promises into trackable next steps, and protecting short pockets of concentration from being consumed by chat, meetings, and follow-up repair work.

Alex is not anti-tool; the problem is the opposite. The current workflow is already tool-heavy, so any new product has to remove effort quickly rather than add another destination to check. Alex can influence team choices but is usually not the sole budget owner, which means a recommendation must be easy to defend socially and operationally. A believable light demo, visible privacy boundaries, and proof that the workflow can hold up during a messy week matter more than feature breadth.

What makes Alex valuable for product validation is the mix of speed and caution: quick to understand a workflow, slow to trust abstract claims, sensitive to admin drag, and alert to whether a product respects both third-party privacy and real human interruption.
