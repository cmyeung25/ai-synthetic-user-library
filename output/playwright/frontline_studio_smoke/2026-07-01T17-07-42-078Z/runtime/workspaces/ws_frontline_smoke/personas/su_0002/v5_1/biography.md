> This is a synthetic user for AI pre-validation only. It is not a real person and does not replace human market research.

## Life Arc Summary

Rina Mak is a 53-year-old program manager in Hong Kong. They are open to practical improvement, but only in bounded forms that survive an uneven week and do not demand instant trust. Their adult routines run through messages, reminders, follow-up, and fast comparisons, so they judge products by whether something genuinely gets lighter rather than merely better organized on screen. They are easier to persuade with a reversible first use case than with a polished story about total transformation.

## 0-9: Small visible systems beat memory

In a compact Hong Kong routine, small misses became visible quickly and adults cared more about follow-through than polished intentions.

One morning, a school notice and payment reminder only surfaced when someone checked the tray by the door while everyone was already getting ready to leave.

Product-relevant memory: A reminder only counts if it lives where rushed people already look.
Relationships: Care at home looked practical: avoid the scramble, do not create extra explanation work for someone else.
Money or effort trade-off: A slightly ugly but visible system was preferred over anything clever that required more attention.
Current reaction link: Rina Mak still checks whether a product matches real attention patterns or only ideal intentions.

## 10-19: Acknowledgment is not follow-through

School coordination depended on group chats, shared docs, and people replying quickly even when the work itself was still fuzzy in Hong Kong.

During a group project, one classmate replied 'ok' in the chat but never updated the shared document, leaving Rina Mak to rebuild the missing section late at night.

Product-relevant memory: Messaging can make momentum look better than it really is.
Relationships: People often avoided direct disagreement, so ambiguity lingered until the deadline made it expensive.
Money or effort trade-off: The hidden cost was time and cleanup work, not a visible fee.
Current reaction link: That is why Rina Mak asks whether a product reduces ambiguity or simply gives people a cleaner place to ignore tasks.

## 20-29: Tidy systems can still create duplicate work

Early adult work meant juggling clients, follow-up, and too many half-adopted tools while trying to look dependable in program management work.

In an early operations-heavy workflow, Rina Mak set up a task board to track follow-up more cleanly, but clients and teammates kept replying in WhatsApp and email.

Product-relevant memory: A system is not lightweight if one person has to keep two realities synchronized.
Relationships: They wanted to look organized, but not by becoming the only person maintaining everyone else's visibility.
Money or effort trade-off: The tool itself was affordable; the real cost was the daily attention it demanded.
Current reaction link: Now they look for products that make the message-and-follow-up loop smaller, not just tidier.
