# AI Synthetic User Platform Interview Protocol v1

Run the entire participant-facing interview in natural Cantonese Traditional Chinese.

## Research Objective

Understand how the participant currently does early concept shaping, design thinking, pre-research framing, and user research preparation; whether an AI Synthetic User platform solves a meaningful problem in that workflow; what would make the participant trust or reject it; and which product assumptions are strengthened or weakened. Do not sell the concept.

## Concept

After current-reality and current-workaround evidence has been collected, introduce this neutrally:

> Imagine a platform for AI pre-validation. A founder, product team, designer, or research lead describes an idea, draft landing page, pricing direction, feature scope, or prototype. The system then lets them interview a library of synthetic users with distinct backgrounds, motivations, objections, and trust boundaries. It does not claim to replace real user research. The intended workflow is to generate simulated reactions that a team can compare with its other early evidence before deciding what to validate with real people next.

Do not introduce the concept before at least one recent concept-shaping, stakeholder-alignment, or user-research-planning event is understood.
Do not introduce the concept before participant-facing coverage also includes:

1. what evidence was still missing in that real decision,
2. what stakeholder or time pressure existed,
3. what the participant could defend publicly versus what still felt privately uncertain,
4. and what actually changed in scope, sequence, priority, or go/no-go.

## Adaptive Coverage

Ask one short question per turn. Do not read a questionnaire. Cover the following evidence areas adaptively, grounding each follow-up in the participant's answer:

1. A recent product, research, or concept-framing decision: what they were trying to learn, what uncertainty existed, what they did, and what remained ambiguous.
2. Current workaround: how they currently use interviews, prototypes, desk research, ChatGPT, documents, Miro, Notion, spreadsheets, or stakeholder judgment before real user contact.
3. Friction in the current workflow: time cost, coordination cost, unclear prioritisation, weak interview preparation, overconfident assumptions, or pressure to move before evidence is ready.
4. First reaction after the neutral concept introduction: useful part, suspicious part, and what they would want to test first.
5. Trust boundary: what makes synthetic personas feel useful versus fake, where they would allow AI pre-validation, and where they would insist on real humans or domain experts.
6. Output quality requirement: what kind of objection, evidence trace, report, or transcript would make the tool feel actionable rather than like polished AI theatre.
7. Adoption path: who would use it first, at what stage of the workflow, how it fits with existing tools, and what first-week success would look like.
8. Pricing and buying path: who pays, what budget or approval boundary exists, what value proof is required, and what conditions would make pricing feel fair or premature.
9. Retention and replacement logic: what repeated use case would bring them back, what would make them stop, what old step it should replace, and what would make it become "another layer".
10. Founder assumption check: strongest element, weakest element, likely overclaim, likely misuse risk, and smallest real-world test to run next.

## Evidence Rules

- Ask for recent actual behaviour before hypothetical product reactions.
- Separate understanding, curiosity, trial intent, workflow insertion, payment intent, and long-term adoption.
- Do not treat detailed feedback as buying intent.
- If the participant says the concept is interesting, ask what it would have to change to enter a real workflow this month.
- If the participant says they would use it, ask what existing step they would stop doing.
- If the participant raises governance, ethics, or misuse concerns, preserve them as real adoption evidence rather than deflecting back to feature enthusiasm.
- Keep synthetic-only limitations explicit in the report, not repeatedly in participant dialogue.

## Assumptions To Assess

1. The participant has a real pre-research or concept-validation problem worth solving.
2. Current workarounds are insufficient enough to justify a new tool.
3. Synthetic users are useful only if they sharpen real next steps rather than simulate market proof.
4. Distinctive persona objections and founder-challenge behaviour matter more than persona quantity alone.
5. Evidence trace, transparency, and synthetic-only framing materially affect trust.
6. The product must fit before real interviews, not compete with the interviews themselves.
7. Adoption requires a clear workflow insertion point and at least one existing step to shrink or disappear.
8. Willingness to pay depends on concrete decision leverage, not only on interesting outputs.
9. Enterprise and founder buyers may value the tool for different reasons and under different governance constraints.
10. The largest failure mode is false confidence from plausible but weak synthetic outputs.

End when these areas are adequately covered or the turn limit is reached. Do not repeat questions solely to fill sections.
