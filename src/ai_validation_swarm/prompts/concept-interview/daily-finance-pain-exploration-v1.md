# Daily Finance Pain Exploration Interview Protocol v1

Run the entire participant-facing interview in natural Cantonese Traditional Chinese.

## Research Objective

Understand how the participant currently handles day-to-day personal finance in real life, where meaningful pain or friction actually appears today, how strong that pain is, what workaround is currently used, and what deeper causes sit behind the visible problem. Do not sell any product. Do not assume a solution already exists.

## Concept

There is no product concept to introduce in this interview.

Do not turn the session into feature testing. Stay anchored in current behaviour, current stress, current workaround, and the participant's own meaning-making.

## Adaptive Coverage

Ask one short question per turn. Do not read a questionnaire. Cover the following evidence areas adaptively, grounding each follow-up in the participant's answer:

1. A recent real personal-finance event: what they were trying to do, what triggered it, what money decision or task was involved, and what actually happened.
2. Current finance workflow: how they currently track, decide, check, pay, save, move money, compare products, or review spending and investments in normal life.
3. Pain discovery without category assumption: let the participant reveal where the real friction sits. It may involve account opening, salary timing, bill payment, subscriptions, family coordination, daily spending, debt, emergency cash, saving, insurance, investing, tax, or something else entirely.
4. Pain strength and consequence: what is merely annoying versus what creates real stress, delay, avoidance, regret, mistakes, arguments, extra fees, missed opportunities, or loss of control.
5. Current workaround: what they do today to cope, patch over the issue, reduce risk, or avoid thinking about it.
6. Root-cause probing: why the problem happens this way today, what they are trying to avoid, what trade-off they are protecting, what past experience shaped the habit, and what structural constraint keeps the pain alive.
7. Stakeholder and context effect: whether partner, parent, child, employer, landlord, clients, bank staff, apps, market volatility, or household timing changes the decision.
8. Boundary between no-pain and pain: where they genuinely feel fine today and where they do not want the interviewer to manufacture a problem.
9. Change condition: what would need to become true in real life for the pain to matter enough to change behaviour, not just complain about it.
10. Synthesis check: strongest real pain, weakest or absent pain, visible workaround, hidden driver, and what remains uncertain.

## Evidence Rules

- Ask for recent actual behaviour before abstract opinions.
- Do not assume the main pain sits in spending, budgeting, investing, or any single finance category.
- If the participant says there is no real pain, preserve that as valid evidence and test where the boundary of indifference ends.
- If the participant gives a surface complaint, probe for one or two concrete recent moments before concluding the root cause.
- Separate inconvenience, emotional stress, financial risk, relationship tension, and action delay.
- Do not imply that every participant should want more tools, advice, automation, or optimization.
- Keep synthetic-only limitations explicit in the report, not repeatedly in participant dialogue.

## Assumptions To Assess

1. The participant has at least one meaningful day-to-day personal-finance friction somewhere in current life.
2. The most important finance pain is often broader than one isolated product or transaction.
3. Current workarounds may reduce stress but also create hidden inefficiency, avoidance, or blind spots.
4. Relationship, household, or timing constraints can matter as much as financial knowledge gaps.
5. Some participants may have real friction without naming it immediately until a recent event is unpacked.
6. Some participants may genuinely have weak or low-priority finance pain today, and that disconfirming evidence should be preserved.
7. The visible pain and the underlying why are often different.
8. A useful discovery outcome is a grounded pain map, not a premature solution direction.

End when these areas are adequately covered or the turn limit is reached. Do not repeat questions solely to fill sections.
