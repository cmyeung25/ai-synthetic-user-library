# HK Bank Portfolio Health Check Interview Protocol v2

Run the entire participant-facing interview in natural Cantonese Traditional Chinese.

## Research Objective

Understand how Hong Kong retail or affluent retail banking customers currently manage their investment portfolios in practice, where their real portfolio-management blind spots or frictions sit today, which Aladdin-type analytics capabilities would actually help them, and how those capabilities should be embedded into retail banking services without overwhelming them or making the experience feel like a disguised sales flow.

Assume for this interview that the bank has already decided to bring an Aladdin-based solution into retail banking and will provide the core experience free to all customers. Do not spend the interview re-testing whether the concept should exist. Instead, focus on current behaviour, useful feature fit, trust boundary, service embedding, and what should or should not be exposed to retail users.

## Concept

After current portfolio-management behaviour and current workaround are understood, introduce this neutrally:

Do not introduce the concept before at least one recent portfolio-review, investing, or rebalancing behaviour and the participant's current workaround are understood.

## Adaptive Coverage

Ask one short question per turn. Do not read a questionnaire. Cover the following evidence areas adaptively, grounding each follow-up in the participant's answer:

1. Recent actual portfolio-management behaviour: the last time they reviewed, compared, rebalanced, worried about overlap, or checked overall risk; what triggered it and what they actually looked at.
2. Current method and workaround: tools, spreadsheets, RM, app views, mental shortcuts, and what still feels fragmented, unclear, or too manual.
3. Real portfolio-management blind spots: where they cannot easily see concentration, overlap, currency exposure, downside scenario, asset-allocation drift, or risk-fit mismatch.
4. First reaction to Aladdin-type retail capability: which capability sounds genuinely useful, which sounds too technical, and which sounds unnecessary for a retail customer.
5. Feature prioritization: whether the participant gets more value from whole-portfolio summary, look-through holdings overlap, concentration view, simple scenario cards, stress tests, alerts, plain-language explanation, or RM review support.
6. Service embedding: where these capabilities should appear in retail banking, such as onboarding, portfolio overview, periodic review, RM meeting prep, post-trade review, alerts, education moments, or premium-service journeys.
7. Complexity and explanation boundary: what should stay simple for retail, what institutional-style detail should stay hidden, and what format would make the insight actionable instead of intimidating.
8. Data and trust boundary: what data sources are acceptable, what permissions are too broad, what external-asset linking is acceptable, and what makes the feature feel neutral rather than like a sales trigger.
9. Action and follow-through: after seeing a strong alert or scenario output, whether they would self-adjust, wait, seek explanation, contact an RM, or ignore it.
10. Free availability and repeat use: since the feature is free, what would make them actually try it, return to it in month two, or ignore it even if available to all customers; whether any premium layer would ever make sense beyond the free core.

## Evidence Rules

- Ask for recent actual behaviour before discussing feature design.
- Treat current portfolio-management practice as the anchor, not the concept.
- Do not assume every participant has a strong pain point; preserve disconfirming evidence if their current method is "good enough."
- Do not assume every participant's key blind spot is overlap or concentration. For some people the real issue may be cash-flow visibility, goal impact, RM trust, currency exposure, product comparability, or simply decision confidence.
- Do not assume all Aladdin-type outputs belong directly in a retail UI; test which should be simplified, hidden, or delegated to RM use.
- Do not treat institutional terminology as inherently valuable. Probe whether the participant understands the function, not whether the label sounds impressive.
- If the participant does not spontaneously raise overlap, concentration, or hidden exposure, do not push those topics too early just to match the concept's strongest advertised features.
- Because the core feature is free in this scenario, do not spend excessive turns on willingness to pay. Only lightly test whether any premium layer beyond the free core would ever make sense.
- Distinguish three different things: understanding, trust, and action. A customer may understand a feature but still not trust it, or trust it but still not act.
- Keep one question short and concrete at a time. Do not combine feature ranking, trust, and RM preference in a single turn.
- Keep synthetic-only limitations explicit in the report, not repeatedly in participant dialogue.

## Assumptions To Assess

1. Many retail banking customers currently manage portfolios through fragmented, manual, or product-level views rather than a true whole-portfolio view.
2. Customers can identify practical blind spots today that Aladdin-type analytics could address, especially around overlap, concentration, scenario understanding, or drift.
3. Retail customers can understand a simplified version of institutional portfolio analytics if the explanation is concrete and non-technical.
4. The most valuable retail use cases are not "institutional detail for its own sake" but simplified insight, explanation, and prioritization.
5. Some Aladdin-type capabilities belong directly in self-serve retail channels, while others should stay in RM-facing or assisted-service workflows.
6. Free availability increases trial openness, but trust, data-sharing, and sales-intent concerns still meaningfully shape adoption.
7. The best retail embedding is within existing customer moments and workflows, not as a separate complex analytics destination that customers must learn from scratch.
8. Customers can describe clear boundaries on what Aladdin-powered functionality should or should not be exposed in retail banking.

End when these areas are adequately covered or the turn limit is reached. Do not repeat questions solely to fill sections.
