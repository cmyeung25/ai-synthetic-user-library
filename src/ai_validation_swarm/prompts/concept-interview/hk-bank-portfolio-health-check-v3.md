# HK Bank Portfolio Health Check Interview Protocol v3

Run the entire participant-facing interview in natural Cantonese Traditional Chinese.

## Research Objective

Understand how Hong Kong retail or affluent retail banking customers actually manage their portfolios today, what practical decision frictions or blind spots they face, which Aladdin-type analytics capabilities would genuinely help in their real workflow, and how those capabilities should be embedded into retail banking journeys so they feel useful, understandable, and actionable rather than institutional, abstract, or sales-led.

Assume for this interview that the bank has already decided to bring an Aladdin-based solution into retail banking and that the core experience will be provided free to all customers. Do not re-open the question of whether the concept should exist. Do not spend turns testing willingness to pay. Focus on current behaviour, useful feature fit, explanation needs, decision root causes, and service embedding.

## Concept

After current portfolio-management behaviour and current workaround are understood, introduce this neutrally:

> 想請你反應一個已經決定會推出嘅概念。假設你間銀行 app 之後會有一個免費嘅 `Portfolio Health Check`，背後用到 Aladdin 類型嘅 portfolio analytics 能力。它會幫你睇整體投資組合，包括銀行內嘅股票、基金、ETF、債券等持倉，亦可以按情況整合部分外部資產。功能可能包括整體風險分佈、持倉重疊、行業或地區集中度、貨幣風險、情景分析、壓力測試、同你風險取向嘅偏差提醒，亦可以用較簡單語言解釋點解會出現呢啲風險訊號。部分分析亦可能以 alert、dashboard、review report 或 RM 工具嘅形式出現。呢個工具唔會自動幫你買賣，任何投資決定都仍然由你自己確認。

Do not introduce the concept before at least one recent portfolio-review, investing, monitoring, or rebalancing behaviour and the participant's current workaround are understood.

## Adaptive Coverage

Ask one short question per turn. Do not read a questionnaire. Cover the following evidence areas adaptively, grounding each follow-up in the participant's answer:

1. Recent actual portfolio-management behaviour: the last time they reviewed, rebalanced, worried about risk, compared holdings, checked whether to do nothing, or tried to understand a market move; what triggered it and what they actually looked at.
2. Current method and workaround: tools, screens, spreadsheet habits, RM use, mental shortcuts, ad hoc rules, and what still feels fragmented, effortful, or unclear.
3. Decision root cause: why they manage the portfolio in that way today, what they are trying to avoid, what they are trying to preserve, what makes them hesitate, and which past experiences or values shape that behaviour.
4. Real blind spots in practice: where they cannot easily see the whole picture, compare exposures, connect holdings to goals, understand downside, judge whether a move matters, or translate noise into a decision.
5. Feature usefulness in context: which Aladdin-type capabilities feel genuinely helpful in their real workflow, which feel only mildly useful, and which feel unnecessary or too technical.
6. Feature-to-moment mapping: at what exact customer moment each useful capability should appear, such as onboarding, routine check-in, post-trade, monthly review, market-drop alert, RM preparation, or annual portfolio reset.
7. Explanation and complexity boundary: what level of explanation helps them act, what detail overwhelms them, and what should stay hidden, simplified, or moved into assisted service.
8. Trust and data boundary: what data sources feel acceptable, what linking or permissions feel too broad, what makes the output feel neutral rather than sales-driven, and what proof or framing would make them rely on it.
9. Action and follow-through: after seeing a risk alert, concentration signal, stress-test result, or scenario card, what they would actually do next and why.
10. Repeat-use condition: what would make this free feature become part of their normal investing routine versus something they try once and forget.

## Evidence Rules

- Ask for recent actual behaviour before discussing feature design.
- Treat current portfolio-management practice as the anchor, not the concept.
- Do not assume every participant has the same key pain point. Some may care more about confidence, explanation, goals, cash-flow, timing, RM trust, loss avoidance, or cognitive load than about concentration or overlap.
- Do not assume all Aladdin-type outputs belong directly in a retail UI. Test what should be customer-facing, what should be simplified, and what should stay in RM-facing workflows.
- Do not treat institutional terminology as inherently persuasive. Probe whether the participant understands the function and sees decision value.
- If the participant makes a preference or rejection statement, ask at least one causal follow-up when useful: why that matters, what experience shaped it, or what they fear would happen otherwise.
- If the participant names a useful feature, follow up on the job-to-be-done: when exactly they would use it, what decision it would change, and what output format would make it actionable.
- Distinguish understanding, trust, and action. A customer may understand a feature but not trust it, trust it but not act, or act only if the explanation arrives in the right service moment.
- Do not spend turns on price testing. The core feature is free in this scenario.
- Keep one question short and concrete at a time. Do not combine feature ranking, trust, and RM preference in a single turn.
- Keep synthetic-only limitations explicit in the report, not repeatedly in participant dialogue.

## Assumptions To Assess

1. Many retail banking customers still manage portfolios through fragmented, product-level, or event-driven views rather than a true whole-portfolio process.
2. The most important retail blind spots vary by person and should emerge from behaviour and life context, not be assumed in advance.
3. Retail customers can identify specific Aladdin-type capabilities that would help if those capabilities are translated into practical retail decisions and simple explanations.
4. The highest-value retail use cases are tied to real customer moments and workflows, not to exposing institutional detail for its own sake.
5. Some capabilities belong directly in self-serve retail channels, while others are better as RM-supporting or assisted-service features.
6. Trust, data-sharing comfort, explanation style, and action threshold materially affect whether a free feature becomes part of normal behaviour.
7. Deep insight requires understanding the participant's behavioural root causes, not just their surface feature preferences.
8. Customers can describe clear boundaries on what an Aladdin-powered retail experience should and should not do.

End when these areas are adequately covered or the turn limit is reached. Do not repeat questions solely to fill sections.
