# Facilitator Quality Evaluation

> This audit evaluates a single synthetic interview and its artifacts, not real participant evidence. Any causal conclusion should remain provisional until tested in human interviews.

Overall verdict: **warn**

## Scores

- neutrality: 3/5
- probing_quality: 3/5
- conversation_naturalness: 4/5
- evidence_discipline: 3/5
- causal_rigor: 3/5
- hypothesis_validation_rigor: 2/5
- synthesis_fidelity: 3/5
- overall: 3/5

## Findings

- [high] The supplied hypothesis was not actually tested on its own stated condition: 'no one clearly owns syncing the latest arrangement.' The transcript shows manual checking and non-automatic updates, but no participant-facing probe isolates whether ownership/responsibility was unclear versus simply separate systems/rules. Despite that, the synthesis issues a causal verdict of 'mixed' and frames `exchange_9`-`exchange_10` as a condition-absent contrast.
- [medium] The closing logic treats `exchange_4` as a present-case for the mechanism and `exchange_9`-`exchange_10` as an absent-case, but the absent condition is not established. A provider proactively notifying the participant does not show that syncing ownership was clear or absent; it only shows one source pushed an update.
- [medium] Several synthesis claims are stronger than the transcript supports. '費用風險存在，但較像次要放大因素' is an interpretation, not a participant-stated ranking across cases; `exchange_8` only says time alignment took longest in the Yilan case. The contradiction claiming the lodging case shows the participant would '仍會反覆核對' also overstates `exchange_10`, which says they checked again but less densely than Yilan.
- [medium] The interview became somewhat repetitive around update-source tracing across contrast cases without adding a direct disconfirmation probe for the ownership hypothesis. Questions in `exchange_6`, `exchange_7`, `exchange_9`, and `exchange_10` all orbit notification source and rechecking, but none asks whether responsibility for keeping arrangements aligned was clear.
- [medium] The synthesis mostly stays disciplined, but `hypothesis_assessment.verdict: mixed` implies more balanced evidential testing than occurred. In a single synthetic validation interview without a clean disconfirmation attempt on the stated mechanism, 'unresolved' would fit better than a quasi-adjudicated causal verdict.
- [low] The interview did stay on changed actions after arrangement disruptions, but some reasoning drifts from travel-arrangement synchronization into broader caution about consequence severity and version mismatch. That is a valid alternative explanation, yet the hypothesis framing remains about sync ownership, which was not equally grounded in changed-action evidence.

## Required Improvements

- Add at least one participant-facing probe that directly tests whether the participant knew who was responsible for updating each affected arrangement, without asking for agreement with the hypothesis.
- Do not label mechanism-present and mechanism-absent cases unless the transcript explicitly establishes those conditions.
- Downgrade the hypothesis verdict from `mixed` to unresolved/weak unless a real disconfirmation attempt isolates sync ownership against alternative explanations.
- Tighten synthesis wording so event-specific salience ('time alignment took longest here') is not generalized into primary-cause ranking beyond the evidence.
- Reduce repetitive source-tracing questions and spend one question on isolating the leading alternative mechanism in a concrete recalled case.
